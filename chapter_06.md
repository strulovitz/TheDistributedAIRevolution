# Chapter 6 — Show Me the Honey: How Everyone Earns

Every system that involves people doing things for other people eventually comes down to one question: who gets paid, and how much?

You can have the most elegant technology in the world. You can have the most beautiful metaphor, the most compelling vision, the smartest AI. But if the economics do not work — if the people involved do not feel fairly compensated for their effort — the system dies. It does not matter how clever it is. People need to eat.

So let us talk about money. Not in vague, hand-waving terms, but in specific, concrete detail. How does money enter the system? How does it flow? Who gets what? And why does the split make sense for everyone involved?

By the end of this chapter, you will understand exactly how every participant in the beehive earns — and why the economics of this system are fundamentally different from anything else in the AI industry.

---

## Nectar: The Currency of the Hive

Before we talk about dollars and euros, let me introduce you to the internal currency of BeehiveOfAI: **Nectar credits**.

Every transaction inside the marketplace happens in Nectar. When a beekeeper submits a job, they pay in Nectar. When a queen bee completes a job, she earns Nectar. When a worker bee processes a task, the worker earns Nectar. The platform takes its small share in Nectar.

Why not just use regular money directly? For the same reason that arcade tokens exist, or that Uber does not hand cash to drivers after every trip. An internal currency simplifies everything. It lets the system handle thousands of tiny transactions per day without the cost and complexity of processing thousands of real-money payments. It smooths out the accounting. It makes the experience seamless.

But Nectar is not play money. It is directly convertible to real currency. You earn Nectar, and when you are ready, you cash it out — into dollars, euros, shekels, yen, whatever your local currency is. Think of it like the credits on a freelancing platform: the work happens in credits, and the credits turn into real money whenever you want.

For beekeepers, the process works in reverse. You deposit real money, receive Nectar credits, and spend those credits on AI jobs. Simple.

The exchange rate between Nectar and real currency is straightforward and transparent. There are no hidden fees, no mysterious conversion rates, no fine print. One Nectar credit has a clear value, and that value is visible to everyone at all times.

## The Split: 65 / 30 / 5

Now for the number that matters most. When a beekeeper pays for a job, that payment gets divided three ways:

**65% goes to the Worker Bees.** The computers that did the actual processing — the ones that received subtasks, ran them through their local AI, and sent the results back — collectively receive the largest share.

**30% goes to the Queen Bee.** The powerful computer that split the job into smart subtasks, distributed them to workers, collected all the results, and combined them into one polished final product.

**5% goes to the Platform.** BeehiveOfAI itself — the marketplace, the website, the infrastructure that makes all of this possible.

Let me say those numbers again, because they are important: **65-30-5.**

If you have ever worked with any other platform — whether it is Uber, Fiverr, Airbnb, YouTube, or the App Store — those numbers might surprise you. Most platforms take 15% to 30% for themselves. Some take even more. Apple's App Store takes 30%. YouTube takes 45% of ad revenue. Uber takes roughly 25%. Fiverr takes 20%.

BeehiveOfAI takes 5%.

Why? Because the platform does not do the work. The platform does not run AI models. The platform does not own the computers. The platform does not split tasks or combine results. The platform provides the meeting place — the marketplace where beekeepers find hives and trust is made visible. That is an essential service, and it deserves compensation. But it does not deserve the lion's share.

The workers do the work. The queen provides the intelligence. They should get the money. It is that simple.

## Why 65% for the Workers?

You might wonder: why do the workers get more than the queen? The queen has more expensive hardware, takes more risk, and does the hardest AI work — the splitting and combining that determines the quality of the final product.

All true. But consider the math.

A queen bee serves an entire hive. When a job comes in with two hundred subtasks, the queen splits it once and combines it once. That is two operations. The worker bees, collectively, process two hundred subtasks. That is two hundred operations.

The queen does two hard things. The workers do two hundred simpler things. But those two hundred simpler things are what actually produce the result. Without the workers, the queen has nothing to combine. Without two hundred computers churning through two hundred subtasks, the job does not get done.

And here is the other side of the math: the queen's 30% comes from the *entire* job. Every subtask processed by every worker contributes to the queen's 30%. So even though the queen's percentage is smaller than the workers' collective 65%, the queen earns her 30% on every single job that flows through her hive.

Let me make this concrete with an example.

A beekeeper submits a job worth 1,000 Nectar credits. The split:

- **Workers collectively receive: 650 Nectar** (65%)
- **Queen receives: 300 Nectar** (30%)
- **Platform receives: 50 Nectar** (5%)

Now, if the queen's hive has fifty active worker bees and each one processed an equal share of the subtasks, each individual worker earns 13 Nectar from this job. The queen earns 300 Nectar. The queen earns more than twenty times what any single worker earns — which makes sense, because the queen invested more, risks more, and provides the intelligence that makes the whole operation possible.

But the workers, collectively, still earn more than the queen — because there are fifty of them, and they did the bulk of the processing. The system respects the collective effort of the many while rewarding the specialized contribution of the one.

This balance is what keeps everyone motivated. Workers feel their contribution is valued. Queens feel their investment is rewarded. Nobody feels exploited. And that is the foundation of a sustainable system.

## How the Money Actually Flows

Let me walk you through a complete transaction, step by step, so you can see exactly how Nectar moves through the system.

**Step 1: The beekeeper funds their account.** A small business owner deposits $100 into their BeehiveOfAI account. They receive Nectar credits equivalent to $100.

**Step 2: The beekeeper submits a job.** They submit a task — say, analyzing two hundred customer reviews — and the system calculates a price based on the size and complexity of the work. Let us say this job costs 500 Nectar.

**Step 3: The Nectar is held in escrow.** The 500 Nectar is deducted from the beekeeper's account but not immediately paid out. It is held by the platform until the job is complete. This protects both sides — the beekeeper's money is safe if the job fails, and the workers are guaranteed payment if the job succeeds.

**Step 4: The job is completed.** The queen splits the work, the workers process it, the queen combines the results, and the beekeeper receives the honey.

**Step 5: The beekeeper approves the result.** After reviewing the work (and optionally rating it), the payment is released from escrow.

**Step 6: The split happens automatically.** The 500 Nectar is divided:
- 325 Nectar goes to the worker bees (split among all workers who contributed, proportional to how many subtasks each one processed)
- 150 Nectar goes to the queen bee
- 25 Nectar goes to the platform

**Step 7: Everyone can cash out.** Workers and queens can convert their Nectar into real money whenever they choose. The money shows up in their bank account or payment service.

The entire process — from submission to payment — is automatic. No invoices. No negotiations. No "please pay within 30 days." The system handles everything. You do the work, you get paid. Period.

## What Determines the Price of a Job?

You might be wondering: who decides how much a job costs? Is there a fixed price list? Does the beekeeper choose? Does the queen?

The answer is: the marketplace decides.

Here is how it works. The system calculates a base price for each job based on its size — primarily, how many subtasks it will generate and how complex each one is. A job that generates thirty subtasks costs less than one that generates three hundred. A job that requires the AI to write creative content might cost more than one that requires simple data extraction, because creative work takes more processing power and time.

But this base price is just a starting point. Different queen bees can set different pricing for their hives, based on their capabilities and reputation. A queen with top-tier hardware, a flawless reputation, and a hundred reliable workers might charge a premium — and beekeepers will happily pay it, because they know the results will be excellent. A newer queen, still building her reputation, might offer lower prices to attract her first customers.

This is supply and demand, the same force that sets prices in every marketplace in the world. When there are many hives available and few jobs, prices drop — good for beekeepers. When there are many jobs and few hives, prices rise — good for queens and workers. The marketplace finds its own balance, naturally, without anyone setting prices from the top.

For beekeepers, this means you always have options. You can choose the premium hive with the stellar reputation, or you can choose the affordable hive that is still perfectly good for your needs. It is exactly like choosing between a five-star restaurant and a good neighborhood bistro — both will feed you, but the experience (and the price) are different.

## Real-World Earnings: What Can You Actually Expect?

I promised this book would be honest, and I am going to keep that promise here. Let me give you realistic expectations for each role.

**Worker Bees:**

Your earnings as a worker bee depend on three things: how powerful your computer is, how many hours per day it is available, and how much demand there is on the network.

A typical home computer — a decent laptop or desktop from the last few years — can process a certain number of tasks per hour. A computer with a good graphics card processes more. A powerful gaming machine processes even more.

I am not going to give you a specific dollar amount, because the system is new and the marketplace has not yet established stable pricing. What I can tell you is the honest framing: think of worker bee earnings as bonus money. It is your computer doing work it was not doing before, earning money that did not exist before. If it covers your monthly electricity bill, that is a win. If it covers your streaming subscriptions, even better. If the network grows and demand increases, those earnings grow with it.

The beauty of being a worker bee is that your downside is essentially zero. You are not spending money. You are not taking a risk. Your computer was idle. Now it is earning. Whatever that amount is, it is more than the nothing you had before.

**Queen Bees:**

This is where the economics get more interesting — and more complex.

A queen bee has real costs. If you are running powerful hardware at home, you paid for that hardware and you pay for the electricity. If you are renting a cloud GPU server, you are paying by the hour. Your earnings need to exceed your costs, or you are losing money.

The good news is that the 30% share is designed to make this viable. A queen who manages her hive well — who keeps her hardware costs reasonable, who curates good workers, who builds a strong reputation that attracts a steady flow of jobs — can run a genuinely profitable operation.

The key skill of a successful queen bee is not technical wizardry. It is business sense. Knowing when to scale up your hardware because demand is growing. Knowing when to scale down because things are quiet. Knowing which jobs are worth taking and which are not. Knowing how to keep your costs below your revenue.

This is, in every meaningful sense, running a small business. And like any small business, the people who pay attention to the numbers are the ones who succeed.

**Beekeepers:**

For beekeepers, the relevant number is not what you earn — it is what you save.

Let us compare. If you use a major cloud AI service to process two hundred customer reviews, you might pay anywhere from $10 to $50 depending on the service and the model you use. If you hire human workers to do the same analysis, you might pay several hundred dollars or more.

On BeehiveOfAI, the same job costs less than the cloud option — because the overhead is fundamentally lower. There are no massive data centers to maintain, no cooling systems to run, no billion-dollar AI training costs to recoup. The computers already exist. The electricity is already being paid for. The AI models are free and open source. The only costs are the small payments to the people whose machines did the work.

For small businesses, startups, non-profits, researchers, and anyone else who needs AI processing but watches their budget carefully — this is the value proposition. Not just cheaper than hiring humans (which it obviously is), but cheaper than the cloud alternatives too. And in the private mode, the cost drops even further — essentially to zero, because the organization already owns the computers and pays for the electricity regardless.

## The Private Mode Economics

Speaking of the private mode — let me spell out the economics there, because they are almost absurdly favorable.

An organization considering the private mode already has the computers. Already pays for the electricity. Already pays for the internal network. Already pays for the IT staff. All of those costs exist whether they use BeehiveOfAI or not.

The only new costs are:

1. **Setup time.** One IT person spends a few hours installing and configuring the software. This is a one-time cost.
2. **AI models.** Free. Open source. Downloaded once.
3. **Software.** Free. Open source.
4. **Ongoing maintenance.** Minimal. The system mostly runs itself once configured.

Now compare that to the alternatives.

**Option A: Cloud AI service.** The organization pays per query, every time, forever. And they send their data outside the building, which for many organizations is not an option at all.

**Option B: Hire more people.** The organization hires analysts, reviewers, researchers to do the work manually. Salaries, benefits, office space, management overhead — the costs are enormous and ongoing.

**Option C: Build a private AI infrastructure.** The organization buys dedicated AI servers, hires AI engineers, builds and maintains a custom system. This costs hundreds of thousands or millions of dollars and takes months or years.

**Option D: Use BeehiveOfAI in private mode.** Install free software on existing computers. Download free AI models. One person, a few hours, done. The computers that were sitting idle at night and on weekends now process the organization's AI work. Total new cost: close to zero.

For a hospital that needs to analyze ten thousand patient records, the choice is between spending six figures on consultants or cloud services — or spending nothing and letting their own idle computers do it overnight.

For a law firm reviewing thousands of documents for a major case, the choice is between billing hundreds of hours of associate time — or having the firm's own computers do the first pass in a fraction of the time, for essentially free.

For a government agency that cannot send data outside its walls under any circumstances, the private mode is not just the cheapest option. It might be the *only* option.

The economics of the private mode are not just good. They are transformative. They turn computing power that organizations are already paying for — and currently wasting — into genuine business value. It is like discovering that the building you have been heating and lighting for years has a gold mine in the basement.

## Why 5% Is Enough for the Platform

I want to address this directly, because if you have any experience with technology platforms, the 5% platform fee probably raised your eyebrows.

Most platforms charge much more because they are providing much more — or at least, they claim to be. Uber provides the matching algorithm, the payment processing, the insurance, the support staff, the massive engineering team. Apple provides the App Store, the review process, the distribution, the billing system, the global infrastructure. These are expensive operations, and the companies argue that their 25-30% cut is justified.

BeehiveOfAI is designed differently. The platform is lean by intention. It provides three essential services:

1. **The marketplace.** The website where beekeepers find hives and submit jobs.
2. **The trust system.** The reputation scores, the ratings, the identity verification.
3. **The payment system.** The Nectar accounting, the escrow, the cash-out process.

That is it. The platform does not run AI. It does not own computers. It does not process tasks. It does not store data. The heavy lifting — all of it — is done by the queens and workers on their own hardware.

This means the platform's operating costs are low. A website, some servers, basic infrastructure. Nothing compared to the billions that cloud AI companies spend on their data centers. And because the platform is open source, even those costs can be shared with the community.

Five percent covers the operating costs and leaves room for the platform to grow and improve. It does not drain value from the people doing the actual work. That is by design. That is the philosophy.

And honestly? If someone looked at this system and thought, "I could run this marketplace better and charge only 3%," they could. The software is open source. The code is free. Anyone can set up a competing marketplace. That possibility — that competitive pressure — is what keeps the 5% honest. It is not 5% because I am generous (though I like to think I am). It is 5% because in an open-source world, charging more would simply drive people to set up their own version.

This is the beauty of open source. It does not just make the technology free. It makes the economics fair.

## The Flywheel: Why the Economics Get Better Over Time

There is a concept in business called a **flywheel** — a system where each part reinforces the others, creating momentum that builds over time. The BeehiveOfAI marketplace is a flywheel, and understanding it helps you see why the economics improve the more the system grows.

Here is how the flywheel works:

**More beekeepers** submit jobs → **More work** is available for hives → **More queens** set up hives to capture that work → **More workers** join those hives to earn money → **More computing power** is available → **Jobs are processed faster and cheaper** → **More beekeepers** are attracted by the speed and price → **More jobs** are submitted → and the cycle continues.

At every turn of this flywheel, the system gets better for everyone:

- **Beekeepers** benefit because more hives mean more competition, which means better quality and lower prices.
- **Queen bees** benefit because more beekeepers mean more jobs, which means more revenue for their hives.
- **Worker bees** benefit because more queens and more jobs mean more tasks available, which means more earnings.
- **The platform** benefits because 5% of a large number is more than 30% of a tiny number.

This is the same flywheel that powers every successful marketplace. Amazon got cheaper as more sellers joined, which attracted more buyers, which attracted more sellers. Uber got faster as more drivers joined, which attracted more riders, which attracted more drivers. The principle is universal: a marketplace that works for everyone attracts everyone, and the bigger it gets, the better it works.

The early days of any marketplace are the hardest — there are few buyers, few sellers, and the flywheel turns slowly. But once it catches momentum, it accelerates. And in our case, the low barrier to entry (free software, existing computers, no technical expertise for workers) means the flywheel can start turning faster than most marketplaces, because the cost of participation is essentially zero.

## A Note About Fairness

I want to end this chapter with something that matters to me personally.

When I designed the economics of this system, I had a choice. I could have designed it to maximize the platform's revenue — charge 25%, like Uber. I could have designed it to favor the queens — give them 50%, since they have the most expensive hardware. I could have designed it to favor the workers — give them 80%, since they do the bulk of the processing.

I chose 65-30-5 because I believe it is fair. Not perfect — no split ever is — but fair in the sense that each participant's share reflects their actual contribution and their actual costs.

Workers contribute the most processing power collectively. They get the most collectively.

Queens contribute the intelligence, take the risk, and invest in expensive hardware. They get a substantial share from every job — enough to run a profitable operation.

The platform provides the essential meeting place and trust system. It gets enough to operate and improve, but not so much that it becomes the one getting rich while everyone else does the real work.

I have seen too many platforms where the company that built the marketplace ends up extracting most of the value, leaving the people who actually do the work fighting over scraps. Ride-share drivers who cannot afford car repairs. Freelancers who lose a fifth of every payment to the platform. Content creators who generate billions in ad revenue and see pennies.

I did not want that for this system. The people whose computers do the work should get the money. The queen who provides the brainpower should be rewarded. And the platform should be grateful for the privilege of connecting them.

That is the philosophy behind the numbers. And because everything is open source, those numbers are not hidden in a corporate boardroom. They are visible to everyone. You can verify them. You can question them. And if the community ever decides a different split is fairer, the system can be changed.

Open source does not just mean free code. It means honest economics.
